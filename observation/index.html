METROCARD

HISTORY

The Metrocard is a magnetic stripe card used for fare payment on transportation in the New York City area.

The system was first proposed by the Metropolitan Transportation Authority in 1983 then designed in 1992 to eliminate the burden of carrying tokens on the subway.

In 2016, the average weekday ridership was 5.6 million. Annual ridership was 1.757 BILLION.

THE SYSTEM

The design considers accessibility to the user. For example, every Metrocard has a slanted edge and hole. The slanted edge indicates the direction of the card to the visually impaired.

The hole towards the bottom left is used by the internal mechanism of fareboxes on buses for alignment and timing purposes as the card is pulled in and pushed back out. Each MetroCard slot in buses has a rubber grip and pin that goes in the hole to swipe the card up and down.

Therefore, as the user holds the card in the right direction, they then swipe with moderate speed through the card reader. As the machine is designed to gather all information fairly quickly, one must not swipe either too quickly or slowly.
